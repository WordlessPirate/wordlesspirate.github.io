Study Methods

When I decided to go back into study I knew it would not be easy. However, I was still hopeful that my (typically) quick ability to grasp new concepts and my (usually) good memory would come to my aid – they didn’t.

Still, with persistence (and the frustration that tends to accompany it) I managed to make some progress. Consequently, my study schedule is about to become a lot more intense. Since I am trying to make this learning experience as practical and enjoyable as possible, I decided to reflect on past and current study methods, so as to avoid previous mistakes.

Practice

I learned this lesson the hard way: when studying a programming language (in my particular case, JavaScript), start practicing it as soon as you can. For me, what seems to be working (most of the time, at least) is a mix of reading/reviewing the available documentation and then practicing on Codewards or Edabit.

Books

There are several great books out there to learn JavaScript but since I can only speak to those I am reading, I can at least tell you that the three titles below offer a great blend of technical concepts and exercises to practice:

  • A Smarter Way to Learn JavaScript

The exercises at the end of each chapter may at times seem a bit repetitive but end up being helpful in grasping the more basics concepts precisely because of such repetition.

  • Get Programming with JavaScript

As you advance in the book, you learn the necessary building blocks to construct a game called “The Crypt” – an interesting and motivating approach.

  • JavaScript for Kids

Aimed at children aged 10+ and their parents, it is a valid and helpful learning tool. Also contains instructions for building simple games (currently building The Hangman myself).

Notes

Regardless of the study field, I find note taking to be extremely helpful. Not only am I committing basic concepts to memory, I am confirming I understood said concepts in the process. I tend to rely on visual memory, therefore my notes follow a certain structure and titles and important notions are highlighted in bright colours. These days, for time saving and accessibility reasons, my notes are all in digital format.

Cheat Sheets & Flash Cards

A consequence of carefully curated and organized notes is that I can easily summarize them and use them for different purposes, such as cheat sheets (for conventions/methods I will need to use time and time again) and flash cards (so I can put myself to the test and see how much of this new knowledge I am retaining).

Since embarking on this journey I have found myself constantly reshaping the way I learn. I would not be surprised if in a few months’ time this whole article needs a rewrite!
