Cause & Effect

I am trying to implement some big changes in my life, one of those being a complete career overhaul. This implies time, a fresh mind and focus. For quite a while, time was not a luxury I had. Now that I do, I find myself battling my own memory.

Did I forget how to learn? Is the way I learn wrong? Yes and no. The change I am aiming for is quite drastic in nature and yes, the way I learn has to change. And it is not that I forgot how to learn, it is just a skill that needs to be exercised once in a while – you can be the most fit person in the world, but nobody runs a marathon without the proper preparation (at least I hope they don’t, the possibility for injury is tremendous).

Come to think of it, and keeping up with this analogy, this might just be the root of the problem: I got injured. I had a lot on my plate and although my body was giving me all the signs, I brushed them off as fatigue and kept going as though I had something to prove. Which I probably did, since I am highly competitive, especially with myself.

Some time ago I attended a Mindfulness seminar. I am not going to bore you with details, but the purpose of mindfulness is to help you be present, in the moment and in your own body, through meditation and other techniques. As our coach walked us through some of the instructions, like “let yourself feel your body” I was surprised to find out how tired and sore my body was. And how that was a reflection of how my mind was feeling.

A lot is said about stress and burn out, but the fact is that very little is done about it. Because stress, in small doses, is a good thing. It pushes your creative limits. Gives you that “ I beat the clock high”. But much like sugar (a topic for another discussion), stress is a silent killer. It does not kill you literally, but it eats at you and robs you of things.

I was recently reading about this topic and some researchers managed to prove that stress, in the long term, shrinks the brain, so each time you have a crisis you are lesser and lesser equipped to deal with the stress. It also makes the heart work too hard for too long. Causes headaches and intense fatigue. It messes up your sleep and your ability to concentrate. Also attacks your stomach and can affect your irritability. For all these reasons and a couple more, stress is linked to depression, also.

The way I see it now, stress is not something to be messed with. If you see yourself in a situation that causes your health to decline, remove yourself from it. Or if you are not able to, at least try and find strategies to cope or manage it.

As for my personal strategies, so far writing, time management and some exercise seem to be helping - but I still have a long way to go.
