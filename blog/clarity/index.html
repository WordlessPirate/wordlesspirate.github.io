Seeking Clarity

There is a quote that I quite like and that it has been on my mind lately:

“Clarity about what matters provides clarity about what does not.”

It is from a book called Deep Work: Rules for Focused Success in a Distracted World, by Cal Newport. (Great book, highly recommend it.)

I have quite big plans for the upcoming months so clarity is not only a necessity, it is a requirement.

Over the course of the last few years I have made some changes - some conscious some unconscious - about the way I deal with health, technology and clutter, among other things. I sometimes purposefully unplug from social tools. I tend to be organized in a way I was not before – I try to not only have less things, but also make sure the things I do have and the way I store them is functional (3 house moves and a country relocation in the span of 7 years tend to put things into perspective).

What I still struggle with is what Carl Newport speaks about in further depth in his book; in my own words, the ability to do more in less time and the ability to complete a task without being distracted by another – as a writer, this sometimes is hard, since you may have a new idea while working on another one. So how do I do this? How do I focus, how to I get the clarity I need?

The answers came in the form of a kitchen timer. You most probably heard about this before: the Pomodoro technique. This time management method developed by Francesco Cirillo is genius in its simplicity. Take a timer, set it for 25 minutes, and focus on your task. Take small breaks in between tasks and a longer break every 4 or so Pomodoro. Adapt it to your needs, if you prefer – I personally prefer 10 minutes for writing and 30 minutes for studying, for instance.

This technique is highly motivating because you get to see immediate results. In my case, the word count for 10 minutes sets as almost doubled. I tend to not feel so overwhelmed by my tasks because I know there is a small break in between them. It is also a nice way to make sure you are not getting lost in the tasks at hand.

For example, if I am focusing on a particular problem for more than 15 minutes without being able to solve it, I might as well move on to something else, get it done, and come back to my more difficult problem at a later time – a few wins will make feel better and hence enhance my probability of success in the more difficult problem.
